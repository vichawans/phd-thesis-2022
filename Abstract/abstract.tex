\begin{abstract}


Aerosols influence Earth’s radiation budget through the scattering and absorption of solar radiation and by serving as nucleating particles for cloud formation, thereby modulating cloud properties such as brightness, coverage, and lifetime. Global Earth system models are an important tool for assessing these effects on climate. Their ability to simulate detailed atmospheric chemistry interactively coupled with aerosol processes opens new avenues for investigating chemistry–aerosol interactions and their roles in the climate system.

Using the UK Earth System Model 1 (UKESM1), this thesis investigates sulfate aerosol formation over the CMIP6 historical period, 1850 to 2014. The UKESM1 output for the Aerosol Chemistry Model Intercomparison Project (AerChemMIP) experiments shows that methane and ozone precursors, which contribute to global warming, also play crucial roles in modifying aerosol formation. By analysing attribution experiments, this thesis shows that methane suppresses the formation of cloud condensation nuclei, further warming the climate beyond previous estimates. Ozone precursors, on the other hand, contribute to aerosol formation, cooling the climate. The seasonal cycle in radiative forcing is then examined, and the fate of sulfur dioxide emissions is discussed in terms of the branching between deposition and oxidation pathways. The radiative forcing of sulfur dioxide per unit of emitted mass is stronger, i.e., it is more negative, in summer because the balance between deposition and oxidation favours oxidation in summer, forming more aerosol particles. Finally, this thesis examines the sensitivity of each process involved in the chemistry--aerosol--cloud--climate interaction and shows that emission location determines the partitioning between deposition and oxidation, with a higher fraction of sulfur dioxide oxidised to sulfate aerosols in eastern North America and South Asia. 

This work contributes to improving our process-level understanding of Earth system models that interactively simulate aerosols from precursors and aims to improve the accuracy of aerosol radiative forcing estimates.

\end{abstract}
