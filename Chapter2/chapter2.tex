\section{UKESM1 and its chemistry and aerosol scheme}  
\label{sec:1.ukesm1}

% what is an esm and climate model
As summarised in Chapter \ref{ch1:title}, numerical models that simulate Earth system processes have grown more complex, incorporating an ever-increasing number of interactions and couplings to represent the Earth as accurately as possible. One such Earth system model is the UKESM1, which forms the basis of this thesis. UKESM1 is intended as a community tool for climate research and is documented by \citet{sellarUKESM1DescriptionEvaluation2019} and \citet{mulcahyUKESM11DevelopmentEvaluation2023}. This section describes the overall model components and provides a detailed description of the chemistry and aerosol components relevant to this thesis.


% ukesm components - should also mention Socrates
UKESM1 comprises a core physical model, GC3.1, and component models representing different parts of the Earth system, each validated and tuned independently to study processes in specific science domains such as ocean biogeochemistry and atmospheric chemical composition. HadGEM-GC3.1 has been tested as a coupled atmosphere-land-ocean-sea ice model \citep{kuhlbrodtLowResolutionVersionHadGEM32018}, with the following sub-components: Met Office Unified Model (UM) for atmosphere \citep{waltersMetOfficeUnified2019}, the Joint UK Land Environment Simulator (JULES) model for the land surface \citep{bestJointUKLand2011}, Nucleus for European Modelling of the Ocean (NEMO) model for dynamical ocean \citep{storkeyUKGlobalOcean2018}, and the Los Alamos Sea Ice (CICE) model for sea ice processes \citep{ridleySeaIceModel2018}. The Earth system components coupled with the core GC3.1 include the Model of Ecosystem Dynamics, nutrient Utilisation, Sequestration and Acidification (MEDUSA) model, which simulates ocean biogeochemistry \citep{yoolMEDUSA2IntermediateComplexity2013}, the JULES model, which also simulates plant physiology and functional types, land use, and the of nitrogen cycling, and the stratospheric-tropospheric atmospheric chemistry scheme (StratTrop) implemented as part of the United Kingdom Chemistry and Aerosol (UKCA) model \citep{archibaldDescriptionEvaluationUKCA2020}, which is coupled with the aerosol scheme GLOMAP-mode \citep{mannDescriptionEvaluationGLOMAPmode2010}. Performance of the coupled model was also evaluated and documented in \citet{archibaldDescriptionEvaluationUKCA2020} and, especially, \citet{mulcahyDescriptionEvaluationAerosol2020} for aerosols.


%The atmospheric chemistry, aerosol and cloud schemes modelled by the UKCA model that is relevant to the sulfur cycle are briefly described here. 
Such coupling among components of the Earth system determines which feedbacks the model can simulate and, hence, its potential applications. The relevant feedback for this thesis concerns aerosol formation and aerosol radiative effects. The natural sources of DMS, a precursor gas for sulfate aerosols, are derived from the MEDUSA component, which simulates oceanic biological activities that depend on surface meteorological processes. In UKESM1, atmospheric chemistry is fully interactive, including chemical feedback among trace gases that play important roles in the oxidation processes driving sulfate aerosol formation. Aerosols are simulated in four aerosol size modes and coupled to radiation and cloud microphysics relevant to diagnosing the radiative feedback, as described below.


\subsection{Tropospheric sulfur chemistry and sulfate aerosol formation within UKCA}
\label{ch2:sec:so4-prod-rate}

The component central to this thesis is the UKCA chemistry model, with its fully integrated stratosphere-tropospheric chemistry scheme, StratTrop, and the coupled aerosol scheme GLOMAP-mode. Both components are covered in model description articles by \citet{archibaldDescriptionEvaluationUKCA2020} and \citet{mannDescriptionEvaluationGLOMAPmode2010}. StratTrop interactively simulates sulfate production, and the production rates are then passed to GLOMAP-mode, which handles aerosol microphysical processes such as nucleation and coagulation.


UKESM1 simulations in this thesis utilised the StratTrop chemistry scheme, in which tropospheric sulfate aerosol sources are either primary emissions of sulfuric acid or secondary production via oxidation of \ce{SO2}, with the latter being the majority source of the total burden. The source of \ce{SO2} includes primary emissions and chemical production from precursor gases such as DMS, which has natural sources. Primary emissions of \ce{SO2} are predominantly anthropogenic from industrial activities. 


Anthropogenic \ce{SO2} emission inventory in CMIP6 follows \citet{hoeslyHistorical175020142018}, and tropospheric volcanic emissions from continuously degassing volcanoes are prescribed over the historical period \citep{andresTimeaveragedInventorySubaerial1998, dentenerEmissionsPrimaryAerosol2006}. Note that all anthropogenic \ce{SO2} is prescribed at the surface. 2.5\% of \ce{SO2} emissions are emitted at the surface as primary sulfate aerosols, accounting for anthropogenic \ce{SO2} emissions that are assumed to enter the atmosphere as sulfate aerosols. 

As the StratTrop chemistry scheme is fully coupled with the aerosol scheme GLOMAP-mode, the chemical oxidants involved in the oxidation of \ce{SO2} are interactively simulated and therefore have their own production and loss mechanisms \citep{mulcahyDescriptionEvaluationAerosol2020}. Sulfate aerosols in UKESM1 are chemically produced via gas-phase oxidation of \ce{SO2} by OH, which produces sulfuric acid that nucleates into new particles, or via aqueous-phase oxidation in cloud droplets. 

Gas-phase oxidation of \ce{SO2 + OH} is a termolecular reaction, Reaction \ref{ch1:eq:so2-oh}, which produces \ce{HSO3}. The free radical \ce{HSO3} reacts with \ce{H2O} to form \ce{H2SO4}. The gas-phase oxidation of \ce{SO2} is expressed in reaction rates as
\begin{align}
    \dfrac{d[\ce{SO2}]}{dt} =  k_{([M],T)} [\ce{SO2}][\ce{OH}][\ce{M}] \label{ch4:eq:so2-oh-prod}
\end{align}

The parameters for the termolecular reaction rate constant, $k_{([M],T)}$, which is a function of the air concentration $[M]$ and temperature T, are given in Table \ref{ch4:tab:so2-oh-reaction-consts}.


\begin{align}
    k_0 &= k_1 \left(\frac{T}{300}\right)^{\alpha_1} \exp{(-\beta_1/T)} \label{ch4:eq:so2-oh-k0}\\
    k_\infty &= k_2 \left(\frac{T}{300}\right)^{\alpha_2} \exp{(-\beta_2/T)} \label{ch4:eq:so2-oh-k_inf}\\
    k_{([M],T)} &= \left( \frac{k_0[M]}{1+\frac{k_0[M]}{k_\infty}}\right) F_c^{\left\{ 1+\left[ \log_{10} \left( \frac{k_0[M]}{k_\infty}\right) \right]^2 \right\}^{-1}} \label{ch4:eq:so2-oh-k_MT} 
\end{align}

The aqueous-phase reactions in UKESM1 require information on cloud droplet populations and acidity, as acidity determines the rates of the \ce{SO2 + O3} and \ce{SO2 + H2O2} reactions \citep{seinfeldAtmosphericChemistryPhysics2016}. Once the reaction rates are determined from Equation \ref{ch1:eq:so2_o3}, the in-cloud sulfate aerosol production is obtained from
\begin{align}
    \Delta S_{\mathrm{cloud}} = F \cdot \left( \dfrac{d[S(IV)]}{dt}\right) \cdot L \cdot N_a \cdot \frac{1}{\rho_w} \label{ch4:eq:in-cloud-sulfate-prod}
\end{align}
where $F$ is the cloud fraction, $ \dfrac{d[S(IV)]}{dt}$ is the aqueous-phase reaction rate, $L$ is the cloud liquid water content, $N_a$ is the Avogadro's constant, and $\rho_w$ is the density of water. In the model version used in this work, global cloud pH is set to 4.0 where the local \ce{SO2} mixing ratio exceeds 0.5 ppb, or to 5.0 otherwise, to described the pH-dependent solubility of \ce{SO2}.

Once oxidation has occurred in both gas- and aqueous-phase reactions, the GLOMAP-mode aerosol scheme parameterises aerosol processes. GLOMAP-mode is a two-moment aerosol scheme that simulates aerosol mass and size independently and treats the number concentration of each aerosol mode separately, accounting for processes such as coagulation and inter-mode interactions \citep{mannDescriptionEvaluationGLOMAPmode2010}. 

In this scheme, aerosol numbers for each mode is represented by a log-normal distribution with a geometric mean diameter $\bar{D}$ covering the nucleation ($\bar{D} < 10$ \unit{\nano\metre}), Aitken ($10 \leq \bar{D} < 100$ \unit{\nano\metre}), accumulation ($100 \leq \bar{D} < 1000$ \unit{\nano\metre}), and coarse ($\bar{D} \geq 1000$ \unit{\nano\metre}) mode. The mass concentration of each component, including sulfate, sea salt, black carbon, organic carbon, and dust, in each mode is also calculated separately. 

Condensation of gas-phase \ce{H2SO4} onto all aerosol modes follows Fuchs and Sutugin (1971), with the accommodation coefficient set to 1.0 for soluble modes. The scheme also simulates the mass tendency of sulfate aerosols due to aqueous-phase oxidation in Equation \ref{ch4:eq:in-cloud-sulfate-prod} \citep{mannDescriptionEvaluationGLOMAPmode2010, mulcahyDescriptionEvaluationAerosol2020}. 

Water uptake by each aerosol mode is calculated with binary electrolyte molalities. Both intra-modal and inter-modal coagulation are included, with the resulting coupled differential equations for particle number concentration solved analytically. Mode-merging is applied after each coagulation-nucleation call to prevent modes from growing outside their prescribed size ranges; fractions of particle number and mass are transferred to the next-largest mode using error-function expressions based on the mode's upper size limit. 

A portion of the sulfate aerosols is lost through deposition. Wet deposition is parameterised using the \citet{giannakopoulosValidationIntercomparisonWet1999} scheme, which accounts for both in-cloud and below-cloud scavenging, and the dry deposition scheme uses land surface types from the JULES model, with a resistance-type model from \citet{weselyParameterizationSurfaceResistances1989}. In the UKCA StratTrop, DMS is not deposited at all, whereas \ce{SO2} and sulfate aerosols are both wet- and dry-deposited \citep{archibaldDescriptionEvaluationUKCA2020}. 


GLOMAP-mode simulates aerosol removal through both nucleation and impaction scavenging. Nucleation scavenging is calculated for large-scale and convective precipitation, with large-scale rain removing particles at a rate equivalent to 99.9\% cloud water conversion to rain over 6 hours. In the modal framework, this process is restricted to the soluble accumulation and coarse modes, approximating the bin-scheme's treatment of particles with dry radii larger than 103 nm. Impaction scavenging by falling raindrops is also included via a look-up table of collection efficiencies based on the mode geometric mean dry radius and a Marshall-Palmer raindrop size distribution modified for rainfall intensity.


Cloud processing is treated in two stages: particles in the soluble Aitken mode larger than an assumed activation dry radius of 37.5 nm are transferred to the soluble accumulation mode, and sulfate mass produced by in-cloud aqueous oxidation is then partitioned between the soluble accumulation and coarse modes in proportion to their respective number concentrations, reproducing the Hoppel minimum in the size distribution.

To simulate the impact of aerosols on radiation, the radiation scheme accounts for aerosol direct and semi-direct effects by computing aerosol optical properties averaged over six shortwave and nine longwave bands \citep{bellouinImpactModalAerosol2013}. Cloud-radiation interactions are simulated only in a warm cloud, using the activation scheme of \citet{abdul-razzakParameterizationAerosolActivation2000}. The aerosol indirect effects, i.e., aerosol-cloud interactions, are sensitive to precipitation treatment \citep{gettelmanAdvancedTwoMomentBulk2015}. The model determines the number of CCN, as theorised by \citet{kohlerNucleusGrowthHygroscopic1936}, using empirical fits to cloud parcel models \citep{mulcahyDescriptionEvaluationAerosol2020}. CDNC impacts \Reff{} and is also used to calculate cloud albedo to represent aerosol indirect effects \citep{bellouinImpactModalAerosol2013}. Compared to single-moment cloud microphysics schemes, double-moment schemes have reduced a range of biases in high-resolution climate models \citep{seikiImprovementGlobalCloudSystemResolving2015}.



\subsection{Theoretical range of sulfate aerosol production rates in UKESM1}

As secondary sulfate aerosol production is the cornerstone of the aerosol budget, this subsection explores the theoretical range of oxidation pathways across variations in temperature, cloud droplet pH, and cloud fraction to confirm the modelled observation. In this section, I investigated the factors limiting sulfate production rates under summer and winter conditions by calculating rates using parameter values from observations or model averages, to assess the importance of each parameter as constrained by location and season. This is to evaluate the UKESM1's parameterisation of sulfate aerosol formation described above.

The equations describing sulfate production includes Equation \ref{ch4:eq:so2-oh-prod}--\ref{ch4:eq:so2-oh-k_MT} for gas-phase reactions, and Equation \ref{ch4:eq:in-cloud-sulfate-prod} for aqueous-phase reactions. The parameters needed for the gas-phase reaction include the concentrations of OH and \ce{SO2}, as well as atmospheric temperature and pressure. Aqueous-phase productions by \ce{O3} and \ce{H2O2} are more complex and require cloud parameters, including cloud fraction, liquid water content and cloud pH, in addition to oxidant concentrations, atmospheric temperature and pressure.

Ranges of sulfate production rates as a function of temperature, pressure, cloud pH and cloud fraction are shown in Figure \ref{ch4:tab:sensitivity-test}. The figure shows that the sulfate production rate decreases with increasing temperature for aqueous-phase reactions, while remaining constant for the \ce{SO2 + OH} reaction. \ce{SO2 + O3} is also found to produce sulfate faster than \ce{SO2 + OH} when the temperature drops below zero \textdegree{}C, a condition more common in winter and at higher altitudes. Pressure plays a role in termolecular reactions as well as in the solubility of gases in cloud droplets by modulating partial pressure. As pressure decreases, the production rates of all reactions decrease. 

For the aqueous-phase reaction, cloud pH strongly influences the \ce{SO2 + O3} reaction. \ce{SO2 + O3} oxidation is approximately one order of magnitude slower than the other reactions, except when cloud pH equals 6.0. In this case, \ce{SO2 + O3} reaction rate is the same magnitude as \ce{SO2 + H2O2}. However, as discussed in the previous section, this pH is not present in UKESM1, as cloud pH is set to 4.0 or 5.0 depending on the \ce{SO2} concentration. This thus limits the importance of \ce{SO2 + O3} in UKESM1. Cloud properties do not affect \ce{SO2 + OH} since it is a gas-phase reaction, whereas aqueous-phase reactions are strongly dependent on cloud fraction, especially when it is below 0.1.

Gas-phase oxidation showed the least seasonal variation, while \ce{SO2 + O3} contributed to the lowest production rates in summer; however, production was comparable to that from gas-phase oxidation in winter, underscoring the importance of this pathway.


\subsection{Evaluation of aerosols in UKESM1}

\subsubsection{Evaluation of sulfur budget}

The sulfur budget has been reported by the UKESM1 for a simulation covering 1981--1998 \citep{mulcahyDescriptionEvaluationAerosol2020}. For this purpose, the Atmospheric Model Intercomparison Project (AMIP), an atmosphere-only configuration of the model driven by observed sea surface temperature (SST) and sea ice, was used, running from 1979 to 2000 inclusive. Hereafter, the simulation is referred to as the AMIP simulation. 

AMIP \ce{SO2} and sulfate aerosol burdens were 0.53 and \qty{0.67}{Tg(S)}. For dry deposition, the AMIP values for \ce{SO2} and sulfate aerosols are 28.98 and 7.10 Tg(S) yr$^{-1}$. As for wet deposition, the AMIP reported 13.38 and 36.0 Tg(S) yr$^{-1}$ for \ce{SO2} and sulfate aerosols. Finally, the AMIP lifetimes of \ce{SO2} and sulfate aerosols are 2.08 and 5.56 days, respectively.

UKESM1 was shown to overestimate \ce{SO2} concentrations by approximately a factor of 2 to 6 in the boundary-layer regions of the tropics and mid-latitudes, as well as in the tropical upper troposphere compared to ground-based observations \citep{hardacreEvaluationSO2SO422021}. The biases in the upper-tropospheric mid and high latitudes are negligible. This may imply that the \ce{SO2} removal process is underestimated or that the natural secondary emissions from DMS are overestimated. 

An in-depth evaluation has shown that the UKESM1 underestimated \ce{SO2} dry deposition, and an updated model has been released to correct this error \citep{hardacreEvaluationSO2SO422021}. Model evaluation against ground-based measurements shows that while the UKESM1 captures historical trends toward decreasing concentrations of \ce{SO2} and sulfate aerosols, it overpredicts \ce{SO2} by a factor of 3 and underpredicts surface sulfate aerosols by 25--35\% \citep{hardacreEvaluationSO2SO422021}. This has led to an update to the UKESM1 configuration called the UKESM1.1 \citep{mulcahyUKESM11DevelopmentEvaluation2023}. When a more realistic dry deposition scheme is implemented, it reduces the model’s overprediction of surface \ce{SO2} concentrations and total column \ce{SO2} but also decreases the overall sulfate aerosol loading, exacerbating the low bias. Our study uses the simulation output from original UKESM1; thus, overestimation of \ce{SO2} is expected.


UKESM1 CMIP6 simulations captured the trends in sulfate aerosol burden relative to ground-based measurements \citep{mulcahyDescriptionEvaluationAerosol2020}. The model reproduces the observed decreasing trend in sulfate aerosol concentration across Europe. Although there is a consistent underprediction of absolute values across all years, the model's predictions fall within the observed variability. The UKESM1 overestimated \ce{SO2} concentrations relative to the Atmospheric Tomography (ATom) field campaign, which measures atmospheric constituents and chemical processes in remote marine areas across both hemispheres \citep{ranjithkumarConstraintsGlobalAerosol2021}. 


\subsubsection{Evaluation of aerosol optical depth}

AOD simulated by UKESM1 against both ground-based measurements and satellite retrievals and found good agreement in trends but underestimated the absolute values \citep{mulcahyDescriptionEvaluationAerosol2020}. The global annual mean AOD trends from the AMIP simulation between 1980 and 2015 show an overall increase in AOD, consistent with the Collection 6 MODIS merged dataset (MODIS C6) satellite retrievals. The absolute AOD is underestimated by 0.03 but remains within the lower end of the satellite AOD range. The spatial distribution shows that the UIKESM1 has higher AOD in the winter seasons of each hemisphere. This is due to high sea-salt emissions, which peak in the winter months. The high bias in sea-salt AOD does not affect our analysis, which subtracts AOD from historical simulation by counterfactual simulations.

% discuss the updated effect of dry deposition from UKESM1 with UKESM1.1
As mentioned earlier, UKESM1 was updated with a new dry deposition scheme and a revised dust tuning, which affects the predicted aerosol AOD \citep{mulcahyUKESM11DevelopmentEvaluation2023}. The new UKESM1.1 predicted a lower aerosol AOD, with a difference of at least 0.02 from 1980 onward, due to higher \ce{SO2} dry deposition, which removes \ce{SO2} from the atmosphere, leaving less to oxidise into sulfate aerosols. This new dry deposition scheme further increases the low bias in sulfate aerosol concentration compared to measurements. The annual mean AOD from UKESM1 and UKESM1.1 over 2003--2014 shows that the global mean AOD predicted by the models is 0.137 and 0.146, respectively, which are lower than most satellite measurements: MODIS (0.162), ORAC (0.170), and Swansea (0.135). These products, their uncertainties, and the details of the evaluation procedure are described in \citet{mulcahyDescriptionEvaluationAerosol2020}. The spatial increase in the AOD in UKESM1.1 stems from the revised dust tuning, which increases the dust AOD over and downwind of major dust source regions. This update is not relevant to \ce{SO2} oxidation. However, AOD decreases in UKESM1.1 over China due to increased \ce{SO2} dry deposition. Our work used output from the original UKESM1; the AOD will have a lower bias in absolute AOD and higher overall sulfate aerosol loading.

\section{CMIP6 experiment design and data archive}
\label{sec:2.CMIP6}

CMIP, an initiative to compare global climate models, has evolved into a significant international multi-model research activity that has shaped climate science research over the last 20 years \citep{eyringOverviewCoupledModel2016}. The initiative aims to better understand past, present, and future climate change using climate models. An essential part of CMIP is the research community's involvement in designing standardised simulations and output formats that facilitate multi-model analysis. 

As such, models developed worldwide routinely participate in this exercise. According to the CMIP organisation literature, the public availability of standardised model output is an integral part of CMIP. The broader climate community and users could utilise the data \citep{eyringOverviewCoupledModel2016}. This thesis benefits from CMIP, and details of data usage are discussed at the end of this section.

\subsection{The DECK experiments}

Since its inception, CMIP has increased complexity and includes more models with diverse complexities and processes \citep{eyringOverviewCoupledModel2016}. Coordination of the project has become more complex and will require more structure moving forward. CMIP6 proposed a standard set of simulations for all models to address this challenge, known as the Diagnostic, Evaluation and Characterization of Klima (DECK). 

According to the CMIP6 overview paper \citep{eyringOverviewCoupledModel2016}, the DECK experiments are designed to provide continuity across past and future phases of CMIP, to be well-established and incorporate simulations that modelling centres perform as part of their own development cycle, and to be relatively independent of the forcings and scientific objectives of a specific phase of CMIP. 

The DECK includes four baseline experiments: a pre-industrial control simulation (\textit{piControl} or \textit{esm-piControl}); a recent historical atmosphere-only Atmospheric Model Intercomparison Project (\textit{amip}); a simulation forced by an abrupt quadrupling of \ce{CO2} (\textit{abrupt-4$\times$CO2}), and finally a simulation forced by a 1\% yr$^{-1}$ \ce{CO2} increase (\textit{1pctCO2}). CMIP6 also suggests a historical transient coupled simulation from 1850 to the present.  The \textit{piControl} experiment serves as the control simulation for the AerChemMIP attribution experiment (see next section), and the historical simulation is used in the same way to provide sea-surface temperatures for \textit{amip}-style attribution experiments in AerChemMIP.

\subsection{AerChemMIP experiments}

CMIP6 endorsed other projects to evaluate models against specific criteria and goals, such as the Aerosol Chemistry Model Intercomparison Project (AerChemMIP) \citep{collinsAerChemMIPQuantifyingEffects2017}.  AerChemMIP aims to quantify the climate and air-quality effects of changes in atmospheric composition from near-term climate forcings (NTCFs: methane, tropospheric ozone, aerosols, and their precursors). NTCFs, especially aerosols, were identified in the IPCC AR6 as the main source of uncertainty in the total anthropogenic ERF from 1750 to 2019 \citep[see figure \ref{fig:1.ERF} and][]{forsterEarthEnergyBudget2021}.

The list of AerChemMIP experiments is quite large, divided into tiers with different experimental protocols. The following AerChemMIP experiments align with the objective of this thesis and are described in detail below.

AerChemMIP proposed transient historical coupled ocean experiments covering the period 1850 to 2014, with the goal of attributing aerosols and ozone to climate change over this period.
These simulations paralleled the \textit{historical} simulation from DECK and differed only in fixing the emission or concentration to pre-industrial values. For example, \textit{hist-piNTCF}, which aimed to attribute the climate impacts of aerosol precursors and \ce{O3} precursor emissions, has emissions kept at the 1850 level; similarly, \textit{hist-piAer} has aerosol precursor emissions kept at the 1850 level. 

AerChemMIP also proposed transient historical prescribed SST simulations that use atmosphere-only configurations with prescribed sea surface temperatures (SSTs) and sea ice. The use of historical SSTs rather than pre-industrial SSTs eliminates the effects of inconsistent background climate states, such as different cloud cover and natural emissions, that could affect aerosol and relative species concentrations. This set of historical prescribed SST experiments includes a wider range of chemical species than the historical coupled-ocean experiments mentioned above, making it vital for this work to investigate the effects of changes in oxidants on oxidation branching ratios. Experiments that are relevant to \ce{SO2} oxidation include \textit{histSST}, \textit{histSST-piNTCF}, \textit{histSST-piAer}, \textit{histSST-piO3} and \textit{histSST-piCH4}. The suffix -piX denotes that the emission of X species is fixed at the 1850 level.


This research focuses on \ce{SO2} oxidation and aerosol formation, utilising data from the AerChemMIP transient historical atmosphere-only prescribed SST simulations \citep{collinsAerChemMIPQuantifyingEffects2017}. These simulations were designed to calculate transient ERFs that drive climate change.  While the simulations were not precisely designed for this goal, their configurations and emissions align well with this research purpose.

\section{Methods for derivation of metrics used in this work}
\label{ch2:sec:defs_terms}

\subsection{Aerosol effective radiative forcing calculation}
\label{sec:ch2:erf}


Greenhouse gases, aerosols, and clouds could perturb the Earth's radiative balance. One way to quantify the effect of each climate forcing agent is to calculate its effective radiative forcing (ERF). The ERF is calculated from the difference in the net top-of-atmosphere radiative flux ($\Delta F$) between the perturbed simulation (e.g. \textit{piAer}) and \textit{histSST} simulation as  $\text{ERF} = \Delta F$

\citet{ghanTechnicalNoteEstimating2013} recommended decomposing the aerosol-induced ERF into components to evaluate indirect effects. The ERF is decomposed into the clear-and-clean sky part, \ERFcsclean{}; the instantaneous radiative forcing of the aerosol (i.e., scattering and absorption), or aerosol IRF; and the cloud radiative effect, \dcre{}. 

To understand the contributions of various processes to the overall effective radiative forcing (ERF), we can separate the ERF due to direct radiative forcing from that due to the instantaneous radiative effects of clouds and aerosols. Following the method of \citet{ghanTechnicalNoteEstimating2013}, the contribution of the aerosol-radiation interactions to the ERF can be distinguished from that of the aerosol–cloud interactions by using a "double-call" method.

% describe double call method
In the "double-call" method, radiative flux diagnostics are first calculated under all-sky conditions and then recalculated assuming no aerosol scattering or absorption of solar radiation. For each call, the model calculates the shortwave radiative flux at the top of the atmosphere (TOA) and a diagnostic TOA flux with cloud neglected. The first call yields all-sky TOA shortwave flux, $F$, and clear-sky, i.e. "cs", flux, $F_{\text{cs}}$. The second call, the aerosol-free or clean-sky, yields clean-sky TOA shortwave flux, $F_{\text{clean}}$, and clean-and-clear TOA shortwave flux, $F_{\text{cs,clean}}$.

This way, the direct radiative forcing from aerosols can be calculated as the difference between the all-sky flux and the clean-sky flux, $\Delta(F - F_{\text{clean}})$. Cloud radiative forcing is then calculated for the cloudy part of the sky by subtracting the clean-sky flux from the clean-and-clear-sky flux, $\Delta(F_{\text{clean}} - F_{\text{cs,clean}})$. Finally, the surface albedo and greenhouse gas effects are calculated from the clean and clear sky flux, $\Delta F_{\text{cs,clean}}$. This could be summarised as follows.
\begin{align}
\label{eq:erf}
\text{ERF} &=  \Delta(F-F_{\text{clean}}) + \Delta F_{\text{cs,clean}} + \Delta ({F_{\text{clean}}-F_{\text {cs,clean}}}) \\
 & = \text{AerosolIRF} + \text{ERF}_{\text{cs,clean}}+\Delta \text{CRE}'
\end{align}

We also note that the definition of transient ERF used here differs from the usual definition of effective radiative forcing (ERF), in which SSTs are specified as a fixed, repeating climatology \citep{eyringOverviewCoupledModel2016}. In contrast, the SSTs in the experiments are prescribed historical SSTs, with all emissions set to historical values. This work defines transient ERF as a global average of the difference in the top-of-atmosphere radiative flux. The average is calculated as a moving decadal mean.
