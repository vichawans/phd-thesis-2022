\section*{Abstract}

Anthropogenic emissions, including aerosol precursors, were historically concentrated in industrialised regions of Europe, North America, and Japan until the 1990s. A rapid decrease in emissions in Europe and North America over the following decade, coupled with rising emissions in China and South Asia, has caused emissions in the Northern Hemisphere to shift equatorward, where oxidant levels are expected to be higher due to greater photochemical activity. Quantifying the effect of emission location on aerosol formation and the processes underlying aerosol radiative effects will improve our understanding of these effects. While there are existing frameworks that describe the relationships between aerosol optical depth and effective radiative forcing (ERF), and between cloud droplet number concentration and cloud radiative forcing, none as yet treat chemistry. 

This chapter extends the framework to include relationships among \ce{SO2} emissions, aerosol optical depth, and the number concentration of cloud condensation nuclei. This addition facilitates quantification of how the location of emissions influences downstream radiative forcing, in terms of these individual processes. Four bounded areas: East North America, Europe, East Asia, and South Asia were selected for analysis of the regional characteristics of the processes involved in aerosol formation and their radiative effects. Using a historical simulation spanning 1850 and 2014, we show that \ce{SO2} oxidation exhibits strong regional characteristics. In Europe, the combination of high latitude and high cloudiness makes \ce{SO2 + O3} the primary oxidation pathway, accounting for 50\% of total oxidation in the region. In East North America, all pathways contribute equally to sulfate production. In East Asia, the relative contributions of the three channels are similar to those in East North America, whereas in South Asia, \ce{SO2} is principally oxidised by OH, with minimal contribution from \ce{O3}. Applying the sensitivity framework, the process sensitivity analysis reveals a linear relationship among all processes across all regions, despite the simulation spanning 165 years and encompassing diverse emission trends, underscoring the robustness of the analysis. The derived sensitivity of reaction tendencies is consistent with pathway partitioning, with \ce{SO2 + O3} as the most sensitive pathway to changes in the \ce{SO2} burden in Europe, with the sensitivity of \qty{38.3}{Tg(S)~yr^{-1}} of sulfate aerosol produced per \unit{Tg(S)} of increase in \ce{SO2} burden. In contrast, only \qty{1.99}{Tg(S)~yr^{-1}} is produced via \ce{SO2 + OH} per the same burden in South Asia. 

As more Earth system models progress towards emission-based configurations, this framework shows promise for understanding climate responses to emissions and the drivers of the physical and chemical processes that shape the effective radiative forcing. Applications of inter-model comparison to quantify process sensitivity, especially for assessing the impacts of future aerosol emissions, are discussed.

\section{Introduction}

% Aerosol radiative effects are complex! This is problematic because it is difficult to study and understand.
Aerosol radiative effects differ from those of long-lived climate-forcing agents such as \ce{CO2}, as aerosols are localised due to their short lifetime, making their climate effects short-term in the absence of sustained emissions \citep[e.g.][]{forsterEarthEnergyBudget2021, szopaShortlivedClimateForcers2021}. Aerosols' radiative effects are complex, as they involve many processes with their own complexities. The complexity of aerosol formation and uncertainty in cloud processes have contributed to uncertainty in aerosol radiative effects \citep[e.g.,][]{bellouinBoundingGlobalAerosol2020}. 


% there is a proposed approach to disentangle the complexity of aerosol radiative effects. That is the process sensitivity analysis. However, this is only studied in terms of global estimates. Another problem is that the proposed analysis is only applicable for aerosol loading, cloud and radiative effects and omits the aerosol formation process.
One method for disentangling the processes underlying aerosol radiative effects is to examine each process independently and determine the sensitivity of each sub-process response to changes to the driver. Process-level analysis in this way is useful and has been used to assess the impact of industrial-period changes on AOD and CDNC \citep{bellouinBoundingGlobalAerosol2020,ghanChallengesConstrainingAnthropogenic2016}. However, this is only studied in terms of global and long-term estimates. The existing analysis is applicable only to aerosol loading, cloud, and radiative effects and omits the aerosol formation process; the formalism and quantitative studies are not available for the chemical production of sulfate aerosols.

% the aerosol formation part is important because aerosol formation relies on the background state of the atmosphere and the background state is greatly localised and a global approach to sensitivity analysis may be misleading and put some of the chemical production process as more important than the other.
Another dimension of the complexity of aerosol-climate effects is that most sulfate aerosols are chemically produced in the atmosphere, so local and background atmospheric conditions determine their production \citep{karsetStrongImpactsAerosol2018}. Drawing on adjacent studies of ozone and nitrogen oxides, \citet{zhangTroposphericOzoneChange2016} showed that ozone production increases when precursors are emitted closer to the equator. \citet{fuglestvedtClimaticForcingNitrogen1999} showed that the climate effect of emissions of nitrogen monoxide (\ce{NO}) and nitrogen dioxide (\ce{NO2}) is determined by the emission region due to the nonlinearities in \ce{O3} chemistry. Similarly, atmospheric conditions influence the efficiency of secondary sulfate aerosol formation by altering radiation, available reactants, and temperature, yet research on aerosol production remains limited.

Historically, anthropogenic emissions, including aerosol precursors, were concentrated in highly industrialised regions, i.e., Europe, North America, and Japan, in the 1980s, and were subsequently followed by rapid industrialisation in China and South Asia \citep{szopaShortlivedClimateForcers2021}. The majority of industrialised regions are situated in the Northern Hemisphere, leading to zonal asymmetry in anthropogenic aerosol precursor emissions and the resulting climate forcing \citep{diaoAnthropogenicAerosolEffects2021}.  

In addition to the hemispheric asymmetry, emissions in the Northern Hemisphere have shifted equatorward, where oxidants are expected to be more concentrated due to greater photochemical activity. \citet{persadDivergentGlobalscaleTemperature2018} employed an atmosphere-only global simulation and noted that \ce{SO2} emitted in a high-impact region, Western Europe, resulted in a sulfate burden twice that of the same amount emitted in a low-impact region, the United States. However, the atmospheric burdens of the aerosols generated by each regional precursor emission were uncorrelated with the relative change in ERF or global mean temperature. This study provided an important insight: the same amount of \ce{SO2} emitted at different locations resulted in markedly divergent sulfate loadings and subsequent radiative effects, but it did not investigate the reasons for this diversity. In regions with dry atmospheric conditions and lower export, the efficiencies of a unit of emitted precursor are higher, suggesting that aerosol lifetime, together with regional export, is vital for determining regional radiative effects \citep{yangGlobalSourceAttribution2017}. 

This chapter aims to provide a process-level understanding of aerosol formation in different geographical regions by suggesting a method for determining the sensitivity of each aerosol formation process to the radiative effect, examining regional differences in sulfate aerosol formation, aerosol and cloud properties, and the radiative effect, and finally quantifying the regional differences in the sensitivities of each process.

\subsection{Estimating the aerosol ERF uncertainty in processes leading to aerosol ERF}

Multi-model estimates of aerosol ERF suffer from limited precision because ESMs predict a wide range of ERF values, as each ESM employs its own approaches to simulating aerosol and cloud microphysical processes \citep{forsterEarthEnergyBudget2021}. Radiative forcing of sulfate aerosol precursors arises from a chain of processes, from the emission of the precursor gas, \ce{SO2}, to the production of sulfate aerosol, the resulting aerosol burden, which then, by themselves, trigger aerosol-radiation interactions or contribute to cloud nucleation, thereby leading to aerosol-cloud interactions. In aerosol-radiation interactions, the sulfate burden influences aerosol optical depth, which in turn affects radiative forcing. In aerosol-cloud interactions, the sulfate burden influences cloud condensation nuclei (CCN) formation, which in turn affects cloud droplet number concentration (CDNC), thereby altering cloud radiative forcing. 

% speaks about uncertainty in the erf and the efforts to constrain the uncertainty
To quantify these processes, \citet{ghanChallengesConstrainingAnthropogenic2016} derived process sensitivities, including the impact of aerosols on the IRF and the relationship between changes in CDNC and ERF. Sensitivity is a valuable concept because it provides a metric for comparing models. Following the notation of \citet{ghanChallengesConstrainingAnthropogenic2016}, which formalised the aerosol-cloud interaction in terms of effective radiative forcing, the aerosol-cloud-radiation chain of processes can be expanded as follows.

\begin{equation} \label{ch5:eq:ghan2016}
    \begin{aligned}
        \dv{\overline{R}_{af}}{\overline{E}} = 
        \dv{\overline{R}_{af}}{\overline{N}_d}
        \dv{\overline{N}_d}{\overline{N}_{CCN}}
        \dv{\overline{N}_{CCN}}{\overline{E}}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where an overbar denotes that the quantity is averaged over a time period long enough for clouds to adjust to aerosol or to reach chemical equilibrium. $R$, and $R_{af}$ are the all-sky, and \textit{a}erosol-\textit{f}ree radiative fluxes, respectively. In this specific case, aerosol-free radiative flux refers to all areas of the sky, including cloudy skies, and excludes only areas with aerosol cover. $N_d$ and $N_{CCN}$ are the number concentrations of cloud and cloud condensation nuclei, respectively. Lastly, $E$ stands for the emission of aerosol precursor gases.


\subsection{Theoretical framework for sensitivity analysis of chemistry-aerosol-cloud-radiation interaction}

Aerosols have been theorised as a climate forcing agent since 1929 \citep{angstromAtmosphericTransmissionSun1929}, but only recently have the chemical production of atmospheric aerosols been included across multiple climate models in CMIP6 \citep[e.g.][]{collinsAerChemMIPQuantifyingEffects2017, kalisorasDecomposingEffectiveRadiative2024}. However, as models now simulate aerosol production with interactive chemistry, the concept of sensitivity needs to be expanded to account for the additional chemical complexity.

To capture the complete chemistry-aerosol-cloud-radiation chain of processes leading to radiative forcing, this Chapter first extends the methods by \citet{ghanMinimalRepresentationAerosols2012} for estimating aerosol ERF, which specifies the additional radiative fluxes for aerosol-free (clean) and cloud-free (clear) sky parts of the atmosphere with Equation \ref{ch5:eq:ghan2016} with work by \citet{bellouinBoundingGlobalAerosol2020} on the aerosol-cloud interaction chain of processes with aerosol-radiation process and rapid adjustments.


\begin{equation}\label{ch5:eq:ghan2016+bellouin2020}
    \begin{aligned}
        \dv{\overline{R}}{\overline{E}} = 
            \dv{(\overline{R}-\overline{R}_{af})}{\overline{\tau}_a}
            \dv{\overline{\tau}_a}{\overline{E}} +
            \dv{(\overline{R}_{af}-\overline{R}_{cs,af})}{\overline{N}_d} 
            \dv{\overline{N}_d}{\overline{N}_{CCN}} 
            \dv{\overline{N}_{CCN}}{\overline{E}} + 
            \dv{\overline{R}_{cs,af}}{\overline{E}}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

As recommended by \citet{ghanMinimalRepresentationAerosols2012}, the radiative flux of the atmosphere is decomposed as $R=(R-R_{af})+(R_{af}-R_{cs,af})+ R_{cs,af}$, where $R_{cs,af}$ is the radiative flux from the \textit{c}lear-\textit{s}ky and \textit{a}erosol-\textit{f}ree part of the sky, i.e. radiative flux that neglects absorption and scattering by both clouds and aerosols. $\tau_a$ is the aerosol optical depth. In other words, from left to right, the RHS terms are the instantaneous radiative flux changes due to aerosols in a hazy sky, $R_{hazy}=R-R_{af}$, cloud forcing, $R_{cloudy}=R_{af}-R_{cs,af}$, and the rapid adjustment of a clean and clear sky, $R_{cs,af}$. Note that the aerosol-cloud radiation term differs slightly from that in Equation \ref{ch5:eq:ghan2016}, in that the radiative imbalance is calculated from $R_{af}$ in Equation \ref{ch5:eq:ghan2016}, while in \ref{ch5:eq:ghan2016+bellouin2020} the aerosol-cloud radiation uses only the cloudy part of the sky, $R_{af}-R_{cs,af}$.

This Chapter contributes to the existing literature by extending the framework to include the chemical formation of sulfate aerosols from \ce{SO2} emissions. The following subsections show the calculation of the chain of processes that lead to aerosol production from precursor emissions for Earth system models with interactive chemistry. In essence, from Equation \ref{ch5:eq:ghan2016+bellouin2020}, the change in aerosol optical depth due to changes in emissions, $\dv{\overline{\tau}_a}{\overline{E}}$, and the changes in cloud condensation nuclei due to emissions, $\dv{\overline{N}_{CCN}}{\overline{E}}$, are expanded.

\subsubsection*{Sulfate burden as a function of \ce{SO2} emission}

First, starting from emissions, i.e. production, of \ce{SO2}, the change in the material balance of \ce{SO2} over time can be written as the sum of production and removal tendencies.
\begin{align}
    \dv{Q_{\ce{SO2}}}{t} 
    & = \sum_i P_{\ce{SO2}}^i - \sum_j R_{\ce{SO2}}^j  \\
    &= P_{\ce{SO2}}^n + P_{\ce{SO2}}^a + P_{\ce{SO2}}^c 
    - R_{\ce{SO2}}^d - R_{\ce{SO2}}^w - R_{\ce{SO2}}^t - R_{\ce{SO2}}^c  
\end{align}

Where, $P_{\ce{SO2}}^i$ denote production of \ce{SO2} from combination of \textit{n}atural ($n$), \textit{a}nthropogenic ($a$), and \textit{c}hemical ($c$) sources. Similarly, \ce{SO2} removal, $R_{\ce{SO2}}^j$, is the sum of wet ($w$) and dry ($d$) deposition, transport out of the region or air parcel, and chemical ($c$) losses.

For \ce{SO2} in steady state, the change in \ce{SO2} burden over time is zero.

% \begin{equation}
\begin{align}
    0 & = \sum_i P_{\ce{SO2}}^i - \sum_j R_{\ce{SO2}}^j \\
    \sum_i P_{\ce{SO2}}^i &= \sum_j R_{\ce{SO2}}^j \\
    \sum_i P_{\ce{SO2}}^i &= \sum_j L_{\ce{SO2}}^j Q_{\ce{SO2}} \label{ch5:eq:dq/dt} 
\end{align}
% \end{equation}

In Earth system models, we can quantify the change in a chemical or atmospheric state, $X$, due to a specified pertubation as $\Delta X = X_{pert} - X_{ctl}$. To do so, two simulations are required: control simulation and the perturbed simulation, which has all the same external forcing except the forcing that is being perturbed. In our case, to quantify the impact of the aerosol precursor emissions in the historical period, the difference between \histsst{} and \sstpiaer{} could be used, such that $\Delta X = X_{\text{\histsst{}}}-X_{\text{\sstpiaer{}}}$. 

X could represent the total change in \ce{SO2} production. In our case, all \ce{SO2} emissions are identical in \histsst{} and \sstpiaer{}, except historical anthropogenic emissions.  
\begin{align}
    \Delta P_{\ce{SO2}}
    & = \sum_i P_{\ce{SO2}, \text{\histsst{}}}^i - \sum_i P_{\ce{SO2}, \text{\sstpiaer{}}}^i \\
    & = P_{\ce{SO2}, \text{\histsst{}}}^a - P_{\ce{SO2}, \text{\sstpiaer{}}}^a \\
    & = \Delta P_{\ce{SO2}}^a \label{ch5:eq:Dp=Dpa}
\end{align}

That is, combining Equation \ref{ch5:eq:dq/dt} and \ref{ch5:eq:Dp=Dpa}, the linear relationship between the change in \ce{SO2} burden due to changes in emission between PD and PI could be written as, 
\begin{align}
    P_{\ce{SO2},\histsst{}}^a &= \sum_j L_{\ce{SO2},\histsst{}}^j Q_{\ce{SO2},\histsst{}} \\
    P_{\ce{SO2},\sstpiaer{}}^a &= \sum_j L_{\ce{SO2},\sstpiaer{}}^j Q_{\ce{SO2},\sstpiaer{}} \\
    P_{\ce{SO2},\histsst{}} - P_{\ce{SO2},\sstpiaer{}} &= \big(\sum_j L_{\ce{SO2},\histsst{}}^j - \sum_j L_{\ce{SO2},\sstpiaer{}}^j\big)  \big(Q_{\ce{SO2},\histsst{}}^a - Q_{\ce{SO2},\sstpiaer{}}^a\big) \\
    \Delta Q_{\ce{SO2}} &= \gamma_1 \Delta P_{\ce{SO2}}^a
\end{align}

where $\gamma_1$ is defined to be the sensitivity of \ce{SO2} burden due to changes in anthropogenic emissions, and is a reciprocal of an arithmetic sum of all the pseudo first-order loss rate constants, $(\Delta\sum_j L_{\ce{SO2}}^j)^{-1}$. $\gamma_1$ could be calculated from the slope of linear relationship between the change in oxidant concentration due to $\Delta P_{\ce{SO2}}^a$ and the change in oxidant concentration due to $\Delta Q_{\ce{SO2}}$.

\subsubsection*{Production of sulfate as a function of \ce{SO2} burden}

To consider the production of sulfate aerosols, let us consider the reaction rate of sulfate aerosol, collectively written as sulfur in the +6 oxidation state (S(VI)), as parameterised in UKESM1, as described by \citet{mannDescriptionEvaluationGLOMAPmode2010}. The production of S(VI) (in \unit{molecules~cm^{−3}~s^{−1}}), written using function notation, i.e. $f(x,y)$ is a function of $x$ and $y$, is given by

\begin{align}
    rate_{\ce{SO2 + OH}}([\ce{SO2}],[OH],[M],T) &= k_1(T,[M])[\ce{SO2}][\ce{OH}] \\
    rate_{\ce{SO2 + O3}}(F,L,[\ce{SO2}],[\ce{O3}], [\ce{H+}],[M],T) &= F \cdot \left( \dv{[S(IV)]}{t}\right)_{\ce{SO2 + O3}} \cdot L \cdot N_a \cdot \frac{1}{\rho_w}\\
    rate_{\ce{SO2 + H2O2}}(F,L,[\ce{SO2}],[\ce{H2O2}], [\ce{H+}],[M],T) &= F \cdot \left( \dv{[S(IV)]}{t}\right)_{\ce{SO2 + H2O2}} \cdot L \cdot N_a \cdot \frac{1}{\rho_w}
\end{align}


where rate constants, $k_1$, depend on temperature, T, and air concentration, [M]. The aqueous-phase productions, $\left( \dv{[S(IV)]}{t}\right)_{\ce{SO2 + O3}}$ and $\left( \dv{[S(IV)]}{t}\right)_{\ce{SO2 + H2O2}}$,  follow Equation \ref{ch4:eq:in-cloud-sulfate-prod} and is a function of aerosol droplet pH, [\ce{H+}], T, and the gas-phase concentration of the reactants, [\ce{O3}] or [\ce{H2O2}]. $F$ is the cloud fraction; $L$ is the cloud liquid water content; $N_a$ is the Avogadro's constant; and $\rho_w$ is the density of water. In the model version used in this work, global cloud pH is set to 4.0 when the local \ce{SO2} mixing ratio exceeds 0.5 ppb, and to 5.0 otherwise. 

The total rate of chemical production of S(VI), $rate_{\text{S(VI)}}$, is a summation of each production pathway. 

\begin{equation} \label{ch5:eq:rate_s(vi)}
\begin{aligned}
    rate_{\text{S(VI)}} = & rate_{\ce{SO2 + OH}}([\ce{SO2}],[OH],[M],T) \\
    &+ rate_{\ce{SO2 + O3}}(F,L,[\ce{SO2}],[\ce{O3}], [\ce{H+}],[M],T) \\
    &+ rate_{\ce{SO2 + H2O2}}(F,L,[\ce{SO2}],[\ce{H2O2}], [\ce{H+}],[M],T)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
    

Reaction rate of X, $\dv{[\text{X}]}{t}$ in \unit{molecule~cm^{-3}~s^{-1}}, which is an intensive variable, can be expressed as atmospheric production tendency, in $P_\text{X}$ \unit{Tg(X)~yr^{-1}}, an extensive variable, by

\begin{align*}
    P_\text{X} =& \boxed{\dv{[\text{X}]}{t}} \frac{\unit{molecules~X}}{\unit{cm^3~s}} 
    \cdot \frac{10^6~\unit{cm^3}}{1~\unit{m^3}}
    \cdot \boxed{vol}~\unit{m^3}\\
    & \cdot \frac{1~\unit{mol~X}}{N_A~\unit{molecules~X}}
    \cdot \frac{M_\text{X}~\unit{kg(X)}}{1~\unit{mol~X}}
    \cdot \frac{10^{-9}~\unit{Tg(X)}}{1~\unit{kg(X)}}
    \cdot \frac{N_{\text{s-in-yr}}~\unit{s}}{1~\unit{year}}\\
    =& \boxed{\dv{[\text{X}]}{t}} \cdot \boxed{vol}\cdot \frac{M_{\text{X}} N_{\text{s-in-yr}}}{N_A} \cdot 10^{-3} \unit{Tg(S)~yr^{-1}} \label{ch5:eq:p_rate_conv}
\end{align*}

Boxed variables or expressions are those that could be obtained from the UKESM1 output. $vol$ stands for the grid volume, $M_X$ are the molar mass of $X$, and $N_{\text{s-in-yr}}$ is the number of seconds in a year. Similarly, concentration of trace gas X, [X] in \unit{molecule~cm^{-3}~s^{-1}}, which is an intensive variable, can be expressed as the burden, $Q_\text{X}$ in \unit{Tg(X)}, an extensive variable, as

\begin{equation}
    Q_\text{X}= \boxed{[\text{X}]} \cdot \boxed{vol}\cdot \frac{M_{\text{X}} }{N_A} \cdot 10^{-3} \unit{Tg(S)}
\end{equation}

Using the conversions above, Equation \ref{ch5:eq:rate_s(vi)} then can be expressed as

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    P_{\text{S(VI)}} = & P_{\ce{SO2 + OH}}(Q_{\ce{SO2}},[OH],[M],T) \\
    &+ P_{\ce{SO2 + O3}}(F,L,Q_{\ce{SO2}},[\ce{O3}], [\ce{H+}],[M],T) \\
    &+ P_{\ce{SO2 + H2O2}}(F,L,Q_{\ce{SO2}},[\ce{H2O2}], [\ce{H+}],[M],T)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

When quantifying the changes in reaction rates due to historical \ce{SO2} emissions in each grid box of the model by subtracting the perturbed simulation from the control simulation, many variables do not change. There is no change in surface air temperature in the atmosphere-only simulations, as the surface temperature is constrained, so changes in air temperature are negligible. Again, assume that the change in oxidant concentration due to the reactions with \ce{SO2} is negligible and [OH], [\ce{O3}], and [\ce{H2O2}] are now treated as constant. Cloud fraction, F, and liquid water content, L, are linked and cannot be linearised. Define a new internal change in cloud, $F' = F \cdot L$. 


\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    P_{\text{S(VI)}} = & P_{\ce{SO2 + OH}}(Q_{\ce{SO2}}) \\
    &+ P_{\ce{SO2 + O3}}(F',Q_{\ce{SO2}}) \\
    &+ P_{\ce{SO2 + H2O2}}(F',Q_{\ce{SO2}})
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The total derivative of sulfate production could be written as

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \dd{P_{\text{S(VI)}}} = & 
    \dv{P_{\ce{SO2 + OH}}}{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \dd{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \\
    &+\pdv{P_{\ce{SO2 + O3}}}{F'}\bigg|_{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \dd{F'} 
     +\pdv{P_{\ce{SO2 + O3}}}{Q_{\ce{SO2}}}\bigg|_{F'} \dd{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \\
    &+\pdv{P_{\ce{SO2 + H2O2}}}{F'}\bigg|_{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \dd{F'} 
     +\pdv{P_{\ce{SO2 + H2O2}}}{Q_{\ce{SO2}}}\bigg|_{F'} \dd{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \\
    % =& \left[ 
    %     \dv{P_{\ce{SO2 + OH}}}{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} 
    %     + \pdv{P_{\ce{SO2 + O3}}}{Q_{\ce{SO2}}}\bigg|_{F'} 
    %     + \pdv{P_{\ce{SO2 + H2O2}}}{Q_{\ce{SO2}}}\bigg|_{F'}
    % \right] \dd{ Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \\
    % &+ \left[
    %    \pdv{P_{\ce{SO2 + O3}}}{F'}\bigg|_{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} 
    %     +  \pdv{P_{\ce{SO2 + H2O2}}}{F'}\bigg|_{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \right] \dd{F'} \\
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

This derivation assumes that all variables are independent.

To take into account the aerosol-cloud interaction and its effects on cloud properties, $\dd{F'}$ could be written as a composite function which is expressed as a chained derivation as follows, assuming that \ce{SO2} burden is the only perturbation.

\begin{equation}
    \dd{F'} = \pdv{F'}{N_d} \pdv{N_d}{N_{50}} \pdv{N_{50}}{Q_{\text{S(VI)}}} \pdv{Q_{\text{S(VI)}}}{P_{\text{S(VI)}}} \pdv{P_{\text{S(VI)}}}{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \dd{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \label{ch5:eq:dF/dQ}
\end{equation}

In another word, it could be said that changes in \ce{SO2} burden has an indirect effect on to cloud properties.

In full, 
\begin{align}
    \Delta{P_{\text{S(VI)}}} 
    = & \gamma_{2,\ce{OH}} \Delta{Q_{\ce{SO2}}}\nonumber\\ 
      & + (\gamma_{2, \ce{O3}}^{direct} + \gamma_{2, \ce{O3}}^{indirect}) \Delta{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \\ 
      & + (\gamma_{2, \ce{H2O2}}^{direct} + \gamma_{2, \ce{H2O2}}^{indirect}) \Delta{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \nonumber\\
    = & (\gamma_{2,\ce{OH}} + \gamma_{2,\ce{O3}} + \gamma_{2,\ce{H2O2}}) \Delta{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} \\
     \Delta {P_{\text{S(VI)}}} = & \gamma_2 \Delta{Q_{\ce{SO2}}} 
\end{align}


Again, $\gamma_{2}$ and its component from each production pathway could be calculated from the linear relationship between oxidation tendency and burden. 

\subsubsection*{Sulfate aerosol production on the sulfate burden}

Next, consider the impact of sulfate aerosol production on the sulfate burden. The derivation is identical to the production of \ce{SO2} burden from production. 
 
\begin{equation}
    \Delta Q_{\text{S(VI)}} = \gamma_3 \Delta P_{\text{S(VI)}}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{sulfate burden on aerosol optical depth}

Aerosol optical depth, $\tau_a$, is a measurable aerosol property that is shown to contribute towards the aerosol direct effect. Aerosol optical depth is the radiative property in which aerosols of all types are used. The total aerosol loading, $Q_{\text{tot}}$, is the sum of all aerosol burdens. 

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \dd{\tau_a(Q_{\text{S(VI)}}, Q_{\text{BC}}, Q_{\text{OC}})} =& \dv{\tau_a}{Q_{\text{tot}}} \bigg\{\pdv{\tau_a}{Q_{\text{S(VI)}}}\bigg|_{Q_{\text{BC}},Q_{\text{OC}}}\dd{Q_{\text{S(VI)}}} \\
    & + \pdv{\tau_a}{Q_{\text{BC}}}\bigg|_{Q_{\text{S(VI)}},Q_{\text{OC}}}\dd{Q_{\text{OC}}} \\
    & + \pdv{\tau_a}{Q_{\text{OC}}}\bigg|_{Q_{\text{S(VI)}},Q_{\text{BC}}}\dd{Q_{\text{BC}}}\bigg\} \\
    = & \dv{\tau_a}{Q_{\text{tot}}} \{\dd{Q_{\text{S(VI)}}} + \dd{Q_{\text{OC}}} + \dd{Q_{\text{BC}}}\}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The aerosol precursor includes OC and BC emissions, and the equation reflects that. However, as shown in Chapter \ref{ch3:title}, aerosol burden is at least 80\% from sulfate burden, so the estimation below could be made.

\begin{equation}
    \Delta \tau_a \approx \gamma_{4a}\Delta Q_{\text{S(VI)}}
\end{equation}

This connects the burden to the theoretical framework in \citet{bellouinAerosolForcingClimate2011} and \citet{ghanChallengesConstrainingAnthropogenic2016}, which analysed the impact of forcing from changes in aerosol optical depth and aerosol-induced cloud on radiative effects. 

\subsubsection*{Summary of the process sensitivity framework}

Ultimately, this chapter adds the sulfate production chain, decomposes d$E$ into production and precursor burdens, and omits the rapid adjustments, as follows. 
\begin{equation}\label{}
    \begin{aligned}
        \dv{\overline{R}}{\overline{P}_{\ce{SO2}}} = 
        \Bigg[
            \dv{\overline{R}_{hazy}}{\overline{\tau}_a}
            \dv{\overline{\tau}_a}{\overline{Q}_{\ce{S(VI)}}} +
            \dv{\overline{R}_{cloudy}}{\overline{N}_d} 
            \dv{\overline{N_d}}{\overline{N}_{CCN}} 
            \dv{\overline{N}_{CCN}}{\overline{Q}_{\ce{S(VI)}}}
        \Bigg]
            \dv{\overline{Q}_{\ce{S(VI)}}}{\overline{P}_{\ce{S(VI)}}}
            \dv{\overline{P}_{\ce{S(VI)}}}{\overline{Q}_{\ce{SO2}}}
            \dv{\overline{Q}_{\ce{SO2}}}{\overline{P}_{\ce{SO2}}}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where an overbar denotes the quantity is averaged over a time period long enough for clouds to adjust to aerosol or for reaching chemical equilibrium. $P_X$ and $Q_X$ denote the production and burden of X, respectively, where X is either \ce{SO2} or sulfate aerosol (\ce{S(VI)}). 

The above equation could be visualised as chains of processes with respective sensitivity, $\gamma_n$, as 

\begin{equation} 
\label{ch5:eq:flow-of-response}
\begin{aligned}
    P_{\ce{SO2}} 
    \xRightarrow{\gamma_1} Q_{\ce{SO2}}
    \xRightarrow{\gamma_2} P_{S(VI)}
    \xRightarrow{\gamma_3} Q_{S(VI)}
    \xRightarrow{\gamma_{4a}} & \tau_{a} 
    \xRightarrow{\gamma_{5a}} R_{hazy}\\
    Q_{S(VI)}
    \xRightarrow{\gamma_{4b}} & N_{CCN}
    \xRightarrow{\gamma_{5b}} N_{d} 
    \xRightarrow{\gamma_{6b}} R_{cloudy}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

In the model estimates, the influence of each process is determined from the difference between pairs of simulations, i.e., \histsst{} and \sstpiaer{}, with the only difference between the simulations being the anthropogenic aerosol precursor emissions. In this way, the external forcing agent is aerosol precursor emissions, $\Delta \overline{P}_{\ce{SO2}}$, and the downstream changes, $\Delta X$, arise from internal processes $X$.

\begin{align}
        \Delta \overline{R} = &\Bigg[ 
        \frac{\Delta\overline{R}_{hazy}}{\Delta \overline{\tau}_a}
        \frac{\Delta \overline{\tau}_a}{\Delta \overline{Q}_{\ce{S(VI)}}} 
        + \frac{\Delta\overline{R}_{cloudy}}{\Delta \overline{N}_d}
        \frac{\Delta \overline{N_d}}{\Delta \overline{N}_{CCN}}
        \frac{\Delta \overline{N}_{CCN}}{\Delta \overline{Q}_{\ce{S(VI)}}}
        \Bigg]
        \frac{\Delta \overline{Q}_{\ce{S(VI)}}}{\Delta \overline{P}_{\ce{S(VI)}}}
        \frac{\Delta \overline{P}_{\ce{S(VI)}}}{\Delta \overline{Q}_{\ce{SO2}}}
        \frac{\Delta \overline{Q}_{\ce{SO2}}}{\Delta \overline{P}_{\ce{SO2}}} 
        \cdot \Delta \overline{P}_{\ce{SO2}}  \label{ch5:eq:final_chain} \\
        \Delta \overline{R} \approx & [ \gamma_{5a}\gamma_{4a} + \gamma_{6b}\gamma_{5b}\gamma_{4b}] \gamma_3\gamma_2\gamma_1   \Delta \overline{P}_{\ce{SO2}} 
\end{align}

$\Delta$ denotes the difference in the temporal and spatial mean of the quantity, $\overline{X}$, between the two simulations, $\Delta \overline{X} = \overline{X}_{PD} - \overline{X}_{PI}$. In this way, all sensitivities could be estimated from CMIP6 models participating in the AerChemMIP simulations, including UKESM1. This framework is not limited to global analysis and can thus be applied to regions to quantify the sensitivity of processes to location.

\subsection{Research gaps and questions}

Despite evidence that aerosol production efficiency and the radiative effects differ across emission regions, quantitative research explaining this phenomenon is limited. \citet{bellouinRegionalSeasonalRadiative2016} uses model simulations to diagnose ERF across different regions, but did not provide a detailed analysis to complement the findings. Subsequently, \citet{bellouinBoundingGlobalAerosol2020} analysed process-level sensitivity for both aerosol-radiation and aerosol-cloud interactions at a global level, but did not include any details on aerosol emission or production. To evaluate aerosol ERF, \citet{kalisorasDecomposingEffectiveRadiative2024} conducts a multi-model intercomparison of atmosphere-only timeslice simulations and transient simulations using output from AerChemMIP. The study included aerosol AOD from multiple models but did not extend to aerosol chemical production or attempt to diagnose model sensitivity. 

For assessemnts, the overall uncertainty is the main focus. While uncertainty in each term from aerosol-cloud interaction to ERF has been shown by \citet{ghanChallengesConstrainingAnthropogenic2016} and from aerosol AOD by \citet{bellouinBoundingGlobalAerosol2020}, efforts to reduce uncertainty in ERF can be made by understanding each process in the aerosol foramtion and radiative effects. It has been shown that the sensitivity of aeroosl aerosol direct and indirect impacts on ERF is a useful tool for understanding the root of uncertainty in ERF. \citet{ghanChallengesConstrainingAnthropogenic2016} laid out a framework for sensitivity of aerosol-cloud-radiation and analysed multiple pre-CMIP6 models. This Chapter will evaluate the senstivity from each process leading to aerosol production in the geographical and global context which has never been attempted before.

\subsection{Sensitivity analysis of aerosol formation, cloud and radiation processes}

In this chapter, sensitivity is calculated by identifying the process driver and response, as detailed in Equation \ref{ch5:eq:final_chain}. For example, to calculate $\gamma_1$, which has \ce{SO2} emissions as the driver and \ce{SO2} burden as the response, the sensitivity is obtained from the slope of a linear relationship between the driver and the response, fitted using a root-mean-square error (RMSE) regression model. 

Each data point is the annual mean of the difference between \histsst{} and \sstpiaer{} in both the driver and the response over a defined region. The slope of this regression model is then taken as the sensitivity of this specific process, with the accompanying goodness-of-fit ($R^2$) and the standard error of the slope for quantitative analysis.

\section{Results and discussions}

This chapter leverages the concept of sensitivity in aerosol-climate interactions to investigate the regional characteristics of sulfate aerosol formation. The analysis is applied to each component of the sensitivity, summarised in Equation \ref{ch5:eq:final_chain}, across four regions, and the results are compared with global values.

This chapter shows the change attributable to aerosol precursor emissions, i.e., the result of subtracting \sstpiaer{} from \histsst{} unless otherwise noted. The anomalous cooling (1960--1990) and near-present (2000--2015) periods are chosen for the analysis of global distribution. 1960--1990 is the period when \ce{SO2} emissions over Europe and Eastern North America peaked, and 2000--2015 covers the rise in industrialisation in East and South Asia, following stringent emission policies in Europe and Eastern North America that led to reduced emissions in those regions.

Firstly, the first section describes the characteristics of parameters that underpin sulfate aerosol formation, including precursor concentrations and cloud properties. Then, the driver and response for each sensitivity term, $\gamma_x$, are explored. A summary of all sensitivities across all regions is provided at the end of the results section.


\subsection{Sensitivity of \ce{SO2} burden due to changes in \ce{SO2} emission [$\gamma_1$]}

The first step in the expansion of impacts of \ce{SO2} emissions in Equation \ref{ch5:eq:final_chain} is a change in \ce{SO2} burden. Figure \ref{fig:ch5:so2_burden_series} shows the changes in annual tropospheric \ce{SO2} burden due to changes in aerosol precursor emissions over the historical period. Overall, \ce{SO2} burden trends in all regions follow changes in emission shown earlier in Figure \ref{fig:ch5:emission_series}. The changes in \ce{SO2} burden over Europe due to aerosol precursor emissions increased gradually since 1875, then grew rapidly after 1950, reaching a maximum of \qty{0.052}{Tg(S)} in 1975. The region saw a rapid decline in burden afterwards to a burden lower than that of 1950. Changes in \ce{SO2} burden in East North America have shown similarities to those in Europe, with a gradual increase since 1875. However, the \ce{SO2} burden in East North America did not increase sharply after 1950. The burden decreased after 1990, falling below the 1950 level. 

In contrast, both East and South Asia showed growth in \ce{SO2} burden since 1950. The rate of increase in burden in East Asia is twice that of South Asia, resulting in a burden of \qty{0.0065}{Tg(S)} in East Asia versus \qty{0.003}{Tg(S)} in South Asia at the end of the simulation. 

While there are clear similarities in how \ce{SO2} burdens grew over the historical period, driven by changes in \ce{SO2} emissions, there are also nuanced differences. For example, at the end of the simulation, emissions in East Asia were \qty{17.2}{Tg(S)~yr^{-1}}, which was three times those in South Asia (\qty{5.1}{Tg(S)~yr^{-1}}). If we assume the relationship between \ce{SO2} emissions and burden is identical in all regions, we would expect the same ratio, i.e. three times, between East and South Asia. However, this is not the case, as the \ce{SO2} burden over East Asia is only twice as much as that of South Asia, and one way to quantify the behaviour of the \ce{SO2} burden response to \ce{SO2} emissions is to use the concept of sensitivity.

Sensitivity is calculated by identifying the process driver and response: \ce{SO2} emissions and \ce{SO2} burden, respectively, for $\gamma_1$. Figure \ref{fig:ch5:emiso2_vs_so2} shows the relationship between \ce{SO2} emissions and burden over selected regions. The global and regional \ce{SO2} burden responds linearly to the emission driver, with a coefficient of determination ($R^2$) of 0.99--1.00 across all regions. All regions exhibit lower sensitivity than the global relationship, and amongst all regions, South Asia has the highest sensitivity of \ce{SO2} burden to emissions. This higher sensitivity captures the differences in the rates of increase in emissions and burden observed earlier in South and East Asia. The values of $\gamma_1$ and all subsequent sensitivities are summarised in Table \ref{ch5:tab:sensitivity}.

The loss of \ce{SO2} determines burden and its response to emissions, with greater loss corresponding to lower sensitivity of burden to emissions, as $\gamma_1 = \frac{1}{\Sigma L^j}$. That is, total \ce{SO2} removal is efficient in Europe and East North America, but less so in South Asia and globally.


\subsection{Regional trends of parameters that underpin sulfate aerosol production}

The next immediate process that follows the change in \ce{SO2} burden is \ce{SO2} oxidation, which, as established in this thesis, responds to oxidant concentration and cloud properties, both of which are variable in time and location. Secondary sulfate aerosols form via either gas- or aqueous-phase oxidation of \ce{SO2}. In gas-phase oxidation with OH, the reaction tendency depends directly on gas concentration and the reaction rate constant. Aqueous-phase oxidation involves more factors, including cloud liquid water content. As with the gaseous precursors, cloud properties vary significantly across regions, further complicating the determination of the tendency toward sulfate aerosol production. This subsection examines both parameters as means of understanding the subsequent oxidation.


\begin{figure}[ht!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics{Chapter5/Figs/ox_conc_annual_mean_histsst_surface_to_5km.png}
    \caption[Annual mean of historical oxidant concentration from the surface up to 5 km over the historical period]{Annual mean of historical concentrations of a) \ce{OH}, b) \ce{O3}, c) \ce{H2O2}, and d) \ce{SO2} from the surface up to 5 km over the historical period in four regions.}
    \label{fig:ch5:oxidant_series}
\end{figure}


Figure \ref{fig:ch5:oxidant_series} shows the trends in oxidants involved in all \ce{SO2} oxidation. Note that these are trends of concentration from the historical transient simulation, \histsst{}, and not the changes due to aerosol precursors. OH concentrations increase over the historical period across all regions. The baseline level of OH in South Asia is double that in other areas due to its lower latitude, which promotes \ce{O3} photolysis and produces \ce{O(1D)}, which can react with \ce{H2O} to form OH. OH concentrations over East and South Asia steadily increase while those over Europe and Eastern North America plateau after 1990. This reflects the trend in \ce{O3} precursors, which promote \ce{O3} concentration and, hence, OH.

\ce{O3} concentration increases globally and regionally over the historical period with less regional variance compared to other oxidants. The average concentration is between \num{6.0e11} and \qty{7.5e12}{cm^{-3}} in 1850 and rises to approximately \qty{1e12}{cm^{-3}} for all regions. The \ce{O3} trends and magnitude in all regions are comparable.

Figure \ref{fig:ch5:oxidant_series}c) shows that \ce{H2O2} concentration over Europe is the lowest, at \qty{4.2e9}{cm^{-3}} at the beginning of the simulation. Meanwhile, the \ce{H2O2} concentration over South Asia is three times that over Europe at the same time, reflecting higher photochemical activity in lower-latitude regions. Over the historical period, \ce{H2O2} concentration increases in all regions, but the increment is muted in Europe and Eastern North America compared to Asian regions. 


Finally, the regional trends in \ce{SO2} concentration follow emissions. The \ce{SO2} concentration in Europe and East North America peaks between 1960 and 1975, then decreases thereafter. In contrast, the increases in East and South Asia begin in 1950 and continue until the end of the simulation period.

The available soluble liquid for aqueous-phase oxidation is determined by the product of the two parameters, termed $cf \times lwc$. Figure \ref{fig:ch5:cf_times_lwc_series} shows the annual mean and global distribution from the surface up to 10 km. Global mean $cf \times lwc$ is constant over the historical period at \qty{0.025}{\gram\per\cubic\metre}. In short, the baseline concentrations and trends of precursor gases that connect to sulfate aerosol formation vary widely between regions, depending on the emission profile, in the case of \ce{SO2}, as well as the photochemical activities over the areas, which determine the photolysis rate of \ce{O3}, OH and \ce{H2O2}. The available cloud liquid water, which determines the tendency for aqueous-phase oxidation, varies spatially.


\subsection{Sensitivity of sulfate aerosol production due to changes in \ce{SO2} burden [$\gamma_2$]}

Sulfate aerosols are formed within the troposphere as the product of \ce{SO2} oxidation. While \ce{SO2} is the main precursor of sulfate aerosols, the local background state plays an important role in modulating this process, especially in determining the partitioning amongst the reaction pathways and also the total reaction tendency. This dependence on local background parameters leads to variability in the sensitivity of \ce{SO2} oxidation across locations. First, we examine trends in \ce{SO2} oxidation by the three oxidants across regions, followed by changes in the total tendency, as inputs to the sensitivity analysis. 


\begin{figure}[ht!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics{Chapter5/Figs/regional_oxidation_series_histsst_sstpiaer_diff.png}
    \caption[Annual mean total oxidation tendency changes over EU, ENA, EA, and SA (\histsst{} minus \sstpiaer{}).]{Annual mean of total oxidation tendency over a) Europe, b) East North America, c) East Asia, and d) South Asia due to changes in the historical aerosol precursor emissions (\histsst{} minus \sstpiaer{}).}
    \label{fig:ch5:oxidation_series}
\end{figure}


Figure \ref{fig:ch5:oxidation_series} shows changes in sulfate formation tendency from \ce{SO2} reaction with OH, \ce{O3} and \ce{H2O2} due to changes in aerosol precursor emissions over the historical period in Europe, East North America, East Asia, and South Asia. \ce{SO2} oxidation trends in Europe broadly follow those of \ce{SO2} emissions, with rapid increases from the beginning of the simulation to 1975, followed by a rapid decrease. Amongst the three pathways, \ce{SO2 + O3} shows the greatest increase at \qty{2.1}{Tg(S)~yr^{-1}}, the maximum production in 1975. It can be seen that \ce{SO2 + H2O2} does not share the same incremental trend with \ce{SO2 + O3}. Compared to the global average and to other regions, high cloud liquid water over Europe facilitates aqueous-phase oxidation (Figure \ref{fig:ch5:cf_times_lwc_series}), making \ce{H2O2} concentration the limit of reaction rate, and not cloud properties, as discussed in Section \ref{ch2:sec:so4-prod-rate}.

Increases in \ce{SO2} emissions cause the \ce{SO2} oxidation tendency in East North America to gradually rise until it reaches a maximum in 1975, similar to Europe. Unlike trends in Europe, total oxidation is distributed almost equally among the three pathways, peaking at 0.95--\qty{1.02}{Tg(S)~yr^{-1}}, with the gas-phase reaction slightly above the other two from 1960 onwards. The higher proportion of \ce{SO2 + OH} relative to that in Europe reflects lower available cloud liquid water, $cf\times lwc$, combined with a higher OH concentration in the region. 

Figure \ref{fig:ch5:oxidation_series}c) shows sharp increases of \ce{SO2} oxidation in East Asia after 1950, with gas-phase oxidation with OH as the primary pathway, reaching the maximum in the near present at \qty{2.9}{Tg(S)~yr^{-1}}. East Asia is located in the same latitude band, which controls the incoming radiation, as East North America and shares similar cloud profiles. Their main difference lies in the evolution of \ce{SO2} emissions over the historical period.

The changes in the \ce{SO2} oxidation tendency in South Asia increase after 1950, similar to those in East Asia. The main difference between the two regions is observed in the gas-phase oxidation, which rose to \qty{2.3}{Tg(S)~yr^{-1}} in 2015. Despite high \ce{O3} and \ce{H2O2} concentration and high \ce{SO2} emission over the region, changes in aqueous-phase oxidation are limited. South Asia has a characteristic limited availability of cloud liquid water, $cf \times lwc$, which constrains aqueous-phase oxidation and favours gas-phase oxidation. 

Comparing the four regions, early increases and emission reduction in Europe and Eastern North America led to a peak in \ce{SO2} oxidation in the 1970s. However, due to differences in background state, such as oxidant levels and cloud abundance, \ce{SO2 + O3} is the dominant channel in Europe, whereas the oxidation tendencies over East North America are equally split between all channels. \ce{SO2} emissions in East North America and East Asia may have evolved differently during the historical period, but their oxidation characteristics are similar. In particular, all \ce{SO2} oxidation reactions contribute nearly equally towards sulfate production. South Asia, on the other hand, shows a characteristically high gas-phase oxidation rate and a minimal \ce{SO2 + O3} tendency, a stark contrast to Europe.

\subsection{Sensitivity of sulfate aerosol burden due to changes in sulfate aerosol production [$\gamma_3$]}

In this section, we examine the effects of the sulfate oxidation tendency on the sulfate mass burden. In GLOMAP-mode, the aerosol microphysical scheme used in UKESM1 simulates aerosol number and mass concentrations independently, allowing the aerosol formation pathway to influence their properties. The key parameters to consider in aerosol-radiation interactions are aerosol mass loading (i.e., burden) and particle number concentration. Aerosols with diameters greater than 50 nm (N50) are relevant to cloud formation as they activate cloud droplet condensation.

Sulfate aerosol production and removal by wet deposition control the total burden. As with sulfate production, sulfate wet deposition, the main removal process, is also controlled by the presence of clouds and precipitation, which vary greatly across locations. Figure \ref{fig:ch5:so2_oxi_vs_so4} shows the response of sulfate aerosol burden to the change in secondary sulfate aerosol production, $\gamma_3$. Europe, East North America, and East Asia share a similarly low sensitivity due to the regions' efficient wet removal. The response is linear, with $R^2\ge 0.90$ across all domains despite sulfate aerosol burden transported beyond the domains, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:ch5:so4_series}.


\subsection{Sensitivity of aerosol optical depth due to changes in sulfate aerosol burden [$\gamma_{4a}$]}

Aerosols are typically 10--100 nm in size, making them efficient at scattering short-wave radiation via Mie scattering. In AerChemMIP, aerosol precursor emissions include multiple precursors for each aerosol type, such as black carbon, organic carbon, and sulfate. Here, I quantify the change in AOD attributable to each aerosol precursor component using timeslice simulations, thereby justifying the assumption that sulfate aerosol is the primary contributor to changes in aerosol properties. 

The timeslice simulations, \textit{piClim-X}, where X is a climate forcing agent, are designed to quantify the radiative effects of that agent. Pre-industrial surface temperatures are constrained to pre-industrial values, and a single forcing agent is used, with the present-day (2014) emission or concentration set for each simulation. In AerChemMIP simulation, aerosol forcing (\textit{aer}) is the combination of organic carbon (\textit{OC}), black carbon (\textit{BC}) aerosol emissions and \ce{\textit{SO2}} emissions. This timeslice experiment is estimated as the breakdown of the last year of \histsst{} simulation.

OC aerosol emissions increase AOD in Canada, Southeast Asia, the Amazon forest, and Siberia, all of which are outside the boundary of the regions of interest of this Chapter. Black carbon aerosol emissions are shown to increase AOD in both East and South Asia. \textit{piClim-SO2} is the primary contributor to the total change in AOD in Figure \ref{fig:ch5:aod_map_piClim}d.

A similar conclusion could be drawn for the East North America region. In East Asia, black carbon aerosol emissions contributed \num[]{0.02+-0.05}, or 10\%, to the increase in AOD, amounting to \num[]{0.19+-0.06}, with the remaining 90\% of AOD coming from \ce{SO2} emissions. Aerosol emission in South Asia is more diverse, with the increase in AOD from OC (\num[]{0.02+-0.08}; 9.1\%), BC (\num[]{0.03+-0.09}; 13.6\%), \ce{SO2} (\num[]{0.18+-0.12}; 82\%) of the total AOD of \num[]{0.22+-0.11}. That is, for present-day (2014) aerosol precursor emissions, \ce{SO2} is the primary contributor to AOD for all four selected regions.

Present-day atmospheric oxidants play an essential role in increasing AOD estimation. There is a non-negligible difference in the atmospheric background state when comparing timeslice simulation pair (\textit{piClim-aer} minus \textit{piClim-control}) and transient simulations (\histsst{} minus \sstpiaer{}). In the timeslice simulation, the control simulation \textit{piClim-control} features pre-industrial surface temperatures and short-lived climate forcing, including \ce{O3} and \ce{CH4}. The transient simulation pair, on the other hand, features time-dependent surface temperatures and other emissions that increase background oxidants, thereby enhancing sulfate aerosol formation. This results in a 33\% increase in AOD in East and South Asia, emphasising the role of oxidants in aerosol formation. Nonetheless, the assumption that \ce{SO2} is the main contributor to aerosol properties is held in both pairs.

We have established that sulfate aerosol precursor, \ce{SO2}, is the main driver for sulfate aerosol, which is the primary type of aerosol in the regions of interest. Figure \ref{fig:ch5:aod_series} shows the impacts of historical change in aerosol precursor emissions on aerosol optical depth at 550 nm. Global mean aerosol optical depth, discussed in Chapter \ref{ch3:title}, shows a steep increase starting from 1950, reaching 0.42 in 2015. The regional mean shows the heterogeneity and local variation in the change. AOD over Europe and East North America is similar in both the magnitude and evolution, with a rapid increase from 1875 to 1975 and a steep decline thereafter. East and South Asia also show similar trends, with AOD increasing through the end of 2014. One notable feature is that the AOD above Asia is 50\% greater than that of the peak of Europe and East North America (0.31 compared to 0.18). In South Asia, this could be due to higher black and organic carbon emissions, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:app2:emioa_series}--\ref{fig:app2:emibvoc_series} of Appendix \ref{app2:title}.


Aerosols' direct impact on the radiation budget arises from aerosol interactions with radiation, with the aerosol optical depth (AOD), $\tau_a$, as the first quantifiable parameter. So far, sensitivities have been calculated using extensive variables such as burden and total tendencies. However, the domain-average AOD is an intensive variable and must be compared with another intensive variable to obtain a meaningful sensitivity. ERF, the next quantity in the analysis, is also an intensive variable. To bridge this change from extensive variables to intensive variables, the sulfate aerosol burden, an extensive variable, is divided by the domain area to convert it to an intensive variable.

\subsection{Sensitivity of N50 concentration due to changes in sulfate aerosol burden [$\gamma_{4b}$]}


In addition to AOD, \ce{SO2} oxidation also affects aerosol number concentration, which is sensitive to both the production pathway and the overall production rate. In UKESM1, \ce{SO2} oxidised in the gas phase produces sulfuric acid vapours which readily nucleate into new sulfate aerosol particles. However, \ce{SO2} that undergoes aqueous-phase oxidation is assumed to react in existing aerosol particles, and does not create new particles. 

\begin{figure}[ht!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics{Chapter5/Figs/regional_n50_series_histsst_sstpiaer.png}
    \caption[Global and regional tropospheric sulfate aerosol burden changes (surface to 10 km) due to aerosol precursor emissions, 1960--2015.]{a) Global and regional time series of changes in tropospheric sulfate aerosol burden due to aerosol precursor emissions (\histsst{} minus \sstpiaer{}) from surface to 10 km altitude. b-c) Global distribution of changes in total sulfate aerosol burden due to aerosol precursor emissions between 1960--1990 and 2000--2015.}
    \label{fig:ch5:N50_series}
\end{figure}


Figure \ref{fig:ch5:N50_series} shows the time evolution of the number concentration of aerosols with a diameter above 50 nm due to increased aerosol precursor emissions. The N50 concentration is shown here, as aerosols with diameters above 50 nm act as cloud condensation nuclei. Globally, N50 increases by \qty{95.9}{\per\cubic\centi\metre}, while within the European boundary, the average aerosol number concentration more than doubled the global average, reaching above \qty{240}{\per\cubic\centi\metre} between 1960 and 1990. Despite a 50\% higher total oxidation tendency and a sulfate aerosol mass burden, the N50 concentration in Europe is in the same ballpark as East North America. This low N50 is likely due to Europe's higher aqueous-phase oxidation, which does not form new aerosol particles. 

A similar pattern in particle density is also observed when comparing East and South Asia. The average increase in N50 number concentration in both regions is the same in 2015, even though the total oxidation in East Asia is double that in South Asia in the 2010s (Figure \ref{fig:ch5:tot_oxidation_series}). South Asia has high levels of gas-phase oxidation, which promotes sulfate aerosol particle nucleation, potentially explaining the higher new particle density per unit of oxidation tendency. Another explanation is that aerosol particle coagulation imposes an upper bound on the aerosol number concentration by increasing collisions and decreasing the number of particles at higher aerosol number density. As with sulfate burden and AOD, while much of the increase in aerosol number concentration is localised near the source, the change in aerosol number is seen beyond the defined region boundaries due to transport. 


Aerosols indirectly modify radiation through aerosol-cloud interactions, which involve numerous cloud-related steps. The first process involves aerosols with diameters greater than 50 nm (N50), which are considered important for cloud droplet activation.  As with the previous analysis involving intensive-extensive quantities, here, the area burden is divided by domain area to express it as an intensive variable.

Figure \ref{fig:ch5:so4_vs_n50} shows $\gamma_{4b}$, the response of N50 concentration to an increase in sulfate aerosol burden per domain area. East Asia is the region with the highest sensitivity of N50 to changes in sulfate burden, with East North America the close second. Both East North America and East Asia exhibit strong gas-phase production sensitivity, $\gamma_{2i}$, proving the point that there is some correlation between sensitivity in $P_{\ce{SO2 + OH}}$ and N50 formation (Table \ref{ch5:tab:sensitivity}). However, South Asia does not exhibit the expected correlation between gas-phase production and high N50 concentration, and no clear explanation is available.


So far, this section has shown that unique regional factors, such as the proportion of aqueous-phase oxidation driven by cloud cover, influence aerosol properties, especially the number concentration of cloud condensation nuclei per unit of oxidation.  


\subsection{Sensitivity of cloud droplet number concentration due to changes in N50 concentration [$\gamma_{5b}$]}

As shown in the previous section, the oxidation location plays an essential role in determining the amount of new aerosol particle formation. This section analyses changes in cloud properties resulting from emissions of aerosol precursors across the four regions of interest during the historical period. CDNC is then used as the response of perturbation to aerosol formation in sensitivity analysis.


\begin{figure}[ht!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics{Chapter5/Figs/regional_cdnc_series_histsst_sstpiaer_diff.png}
    \caption[Global and regional CDNC changes (surface to 10 km) due to aerosol precursor emissions, 1960--2015.]{a) Global and regional time series of changes in cloud droplet number concentration (CDNC) due to aerosol precursor emissions (\histsst{} minus \sstpiaer{}) from surface to 10 km altitude. b-c) Global distribution of changes in CDNC due to aerosol precursor emissions between 1960--1990 and 2000--2015.}
    \label{fig:ch5:cdnc_series}
\end{figure}

With increased aerosol number, cloud droplet number concentration would increase as there are more nuclei for cloud droplets to condense onto. Figure \ref{fig:ch5:cdnc_series} shows the historical changes in average cloud droplet number concentration (CDNC) below 10 km due to increases in aerosol precursor emissions. Globally, aerosol precursors add \qty{32.8}{\per\cubic\centi\metre} of CDNC, which maximises at 1975 and stabilises until 2015, as also discussed in Chapter \ref{ch3:title}. 

Regionally in Europe, CDNC increases and plateaus at \qty{52.4}{\per\cubic\centi\metre} in 1910, then rises again during the pothole period between 1960 and 1990 to \qty{94.1}{\per\cubic\centi\metre}. Thereafter, CDNC over Europe decreases rapidly, reaching only slightly above the global mean of \qty{40.1}{\per\cubic\centi\metre} in the present day.

CDNC over East North America shows the same rate of increase as in Europe during the initial simulation years, from 1850 to 1910, but stabilises at a concentration 30\% higher, \qty{96.3}{\per\cubic\centi\metre}, than in Europe during the same period. Despite lower total oxidation in East North America than in Europe, CDNC over East North America shows a larger increase, consistent with the assumption that a greater fraction of gas-phase oxidation leads to more CCN formation and thus greater changes in cloud properties. 

An increase in aerosol precursor emissions over East Asia leads to a rapid rise in CDNC from 1950 onward, which then slows slightly after 1990 and reaches a present-day maximum of \qty{143.4}{\per\cubic\centi\metre}, 25\% higher than the highest level in East North America. This relationship is expected, as the N50 concentration over East Asia is almost twice that over East North America during the same period.

In South Asia, CDNC's response to the rise in aerosol precursor emissions resembles that in East Asia, with the same rate of change since 1950. Unlike in East Asia, the rate of increase in CDNC does not dampen in 1990, leading to a substantial difference in the near-present of \qty{194}{\per\cubic\centi\metre}.

The impact of aerosol precursor emissions extends far beyond their source regions, especially during peak emission periods over Europe and East North America. Figure \ref{fig:ch5:cdnc_series}b shows that the increase in CDNC covers the whole of the Eurasian continent and the entirety of North America. The impact of East and South Asian emissions is concentrated in the regions where they are emitted. CDNC changes are predominantly observed over continental areas. 

As the cloud droplet number increases with aerosol number, each cloud droplet shrinks in size, and the cloud droplet effective radius (\Reff{}) reflects these changes. Figure \ref{fig:ch5:reff_series} shows the impacts of the historical increase in aerosol precursor emissions on \Reff{} averaged in four regions below 10 km. The global mean \Reff{} decreases, with greater decreases regionally. In addition to cloud microphysical properties, aerosols affect cloud optical properties, as shown by changes in cloud optical depth (Figure \ref{fig:ch5:cod_series}). Cloud optical depths increase globally, with larger changes near sources of aerosol precursors.

    \centering
    \includegraphics{Chapter5/Figs/sensitivity_dn50_dcdnc_histsst_sstpiaer.png}
    \caption[$\gamma_{5b}$: CDNC response to N50 concentration changes across regional and global domains.]{Response of mean cloud droplet number concentration (CDNC; $N_d$) to changes in N50 concentration, $\gamma_{5b}$. Each data point is the difference between the annual means of the perturbed (\histsst{}) and control (\sstpiaer{}) simulations, with the driver plotted against the response for the four regional domains and the global domain.}
    \label{fig:ch5:n50_vs_cdnc}

More numerous CCN are directly linked to a greater concentration of cloud droplets, as shown by modelling and observations \citep{jordanHowWellAre2024, virtanenHighSensitivityCloud2025}. Figure \ref{fig:ch5:n50_vs_cdnc} shows the response of CDNC due to changes in N50 concentration. The global and regional relationships between N50 and cloud droplet number concentrations (CDNC) are linear, with $R\ge0.95$. The sensitivities, $\gamma_{5b}$, from the global and regional analyses lie within the same range, indicating that location does not substantially influence this process.

This section has shown that historical changes in aerosol precursor emissions alter cloud microphysical and optical properties with regional signatures. Europe shows lower CDNC sensitivity to total oxidation because a smaller fraction of \ce{SO2} is oxidised in the gas phase. Meanwhile, South Asia shows greater changes in cloud properties due to higher gas-phase oxidation of \ce{SO2}. The susceptibility of CDNC to CCN density is one of the major factors controlling the highly uncertain change in the amount of solar radiation reflected by clouds, which is quantified by cloud optical depth, when aerosol emissions are perturbed. 


\subsection{Sensitivity of ERF due to changes in AOD and in CDNC [$\gamma_{5a}$ and $\gamma_{6b}$]}

Radiative forcing by aerosols is the most important anthropogenic cooling component of the anthropogenic emissions, and aerosol interactions remain the largest source of uncertainty in radiative forcing estimates \citep{forsterEarthEnergyBudget2021, bellouinBoundingGlobalAerosol2020}. As with the preceding Chapters, aerosol effective radiative forcing (ERF) is decomposed into cloud radiative effects (\dcre{}), aerosol direct effect (\irf), and the albedo plus clean-and-clear-sky component (\ERFcsclean). The sensitivities of ERF due to aerosol-radiation and aerosol-cloud interactions are quantified in this section.


\begin{figure}[ht!]

    \label{fig:ch5:erf_series}
\end{figure}

Aerosols' effects on radiative imbalance at the top of the atmosphere are shown in Figure \ref{fig:ch5:erf_series} as a 10-year rolling annual mean ERF. Globally, aerosol precursors induce a negative ERF, meaning that more radiation leaves the atmosphere than enters it. Negative ERF induces this global deficit in many regions of the Earth. The global distribution of changes in ERF (Figure \ref{fig:ch5:erf_series}b-c) illustrates that the impact of aerosol precursor emissions extends far beyond the emission sources.  Nevertheless, ERF changes are most substantial near the source, so trend and correlation analyses are informative.

Over the defined Europe region, ERF is the most negative between 1960 and 1990 at \qty{-4.39}{W~m^{-2}} with the aerosol impact reaching beyond the drawn boundary into the northeastern region of the Eurasia continent, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:ch5:erf_series}. As aerosol precursor emissions cede in the area, the ERF becomes less negative, at \qty{-2.0}{W~m^{-2}} in 2000--2015.

As in Europe, ERF over East North America is most negative between 1960 and 1990 due to elevated emissions of aerosol precursors, with the coverage of negative aerosol forcing extending far from the source regions into the North Atlantic Ocean and possibly Europe. However, as Europe's emissions are elevated during the same period, it is not possible to disentangle the overlap without simulations that target emissions from each region. 


The average ERF over the East Asia region increases over the historical period, reaching \qty{-3.95}{W~m^{-2}} between 2000 and 2015. However, the ERF in the South Asia region is relatively small, despite evidence of a substantial decrease in \Reff{}. That is, the aerosol ERF over this region is insensitive to changes in cloud properties. However, the lack of ERF response over South Asia may be due to methodological limitations. Just beyond the region boundary, Figure \ref{fig:ch5:erf_series}c) shows a strong negative aerosol ERF in the Indian Ocean region, which may imply that aerosol from South Asia impacts the ERF but not directly at the emission sources.

The aerosol ERF in each region is decomposed into cloud radiative effect (\dcre{}), aerosol direct effects (\irf{}) and a combination of surface albedo plus clear-sky-aerosol-free (\ERFcsclean{}) in Figure \ref{fig:ch5:erf_series_by_region}. When aerosol precursor emissions decrease over the region between 2000 and 2015, the total ERF reduced from \qty{-4.8}{W~m^{-2}} to \qty{-2.2}{W~m^{-2}}.

Due to high BC aerosol emission, the \ERFcsclean{} is distinctively warming compared to other regions.

The extent to which aerosol precursor emissions affect CRE extends far beyond the emission regions, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:ch5:cre_series}. This breakdown of the relationship shows the limits of using regional boundaries to limit the impact of the forcing agent. Nevertheless, apart from South Asia, global and regional values of $\gamma_{6b}$ fall within the same range, indicating that location is not significant for the cloud-radiative effect.

The principal finding from this analysis is that location plays a major role in determining sulfate aerosol formation, as apparent in diversed $\gamma_{2}$ and its components, $\gamma_{2,OH}$ (Table \ref{ch5:tab:sensitivity}a), $\gamma_{2,\ce{O3}}$, and $\gamma_{2,\ce{H2O2}}$ (Table \ref{ch5:tab:sensitivity}b). $\gamma_2$, which represents the sensitivity of total sulfate aerosol production to the available \ce{SO2} burden, shows that sulfate production is least efficient in Europe, and this is mainly driven by the lack of sensitivity to oxidation with OH. Similarly, \ce{SO2 + OH} is the most prominent in South Asia, which also features the greatest total sensitivity in sulfate production, $\gamma_2$ of \qty{9.83e1}{(Tg(S)~yr^{-1})~(Tg(S))^{-1}}

This sensitivity analysis performs well globally, with a strong goodness-of-fit ($R^2 > 0.91$) across all processes, demonstrating potential applicability beyond UKESM1, especially for multi-model comparisons. The cross-model intercomparison would diagnose differences in chemistry and aerosol processes and yield tighter bounds on model-derived uncertainty in aerosol-climate interactions. However, using a boundary to estimate regional sensitivities has limitations, as evidenced by the lower $R^2$ values for non-local processes, such as aerosol-radiation interaction ($\gamma_{5a}$ ) and cloud radiative effects ($\gamma_{6b}$). 

Existing literature on process sensitivity in models is limited to cloud processes. \citet{virtanenHighSensitivityCloud2025} quantified the sensitivity, referred to as susceptibility, S, of cloud droplet number concentration to CCN concentration by comparing four Earth system models, including UKESM1, with in-situ and satellite observations. Compared to measurements, UKESM1 showed lower CDNC sensitivity to CCN in the semi-urban area but greater sensitivity in the Arctic region. For the semi-urban region, UKESM1 is the second-most sensitive of the four models, placing it among the more sensitive ESMs included in the study by \citet{virtanenHighSensitivityCloud2025}.


\section{Conclusions}

Based on the analysis and conceptual framework of process sensitivities presented in equation \ref{ch5:eq:flow-of-response}, this Chapter has examined the role of \ce{SO2} emission location in sulfate aerosol formation, cloud properties, and the resulting direct and indirect radiative effects of aerosols. Emission regions play a significant role in determining aerosol formation, which in turn has cascading impacts on radiation. Four regions were focused on in this Chapter: Europe, East North America, East Asia, and South Asia. 

\ce{SO2} emission trends evolved over the historical period, starting with a rapid increase across Europe that persisted until 1970, after which they declined just as rapidly. In this case, the combination of high latitude and high cloudiness in this region makes \ce{SO2 + O3} the primary oxidation pathway, accounting for half of the total oxidation and setting it apart from other regions. In East North America, the proportion of \ce{SO2 + OH} oxidation is larger, with all pathways contributing equally to sulfate production. East Asia exhibits a partitioning similar to that of East North America, whereas South Asia shows a uniquely high OH oxidation rate, with negligible \ce{SO2 + O3} oxidation. 

These findings could be summarised in terms of process sensitivities. The sensitivity of reaction tendencies quantitatively confirms pathway partitioning, with \ce{SO2 + O3} as the most sensitive pathway to changes in the \ce{SO2} burden in Europe. The concept of process sensitivity could be used to compare across regions. The total sulfate aerosol production over Europe is the least sensitive to increases in \ce{SO2} burden, while East North America and South Asia are the most sensitive.


Crucially, this chapter highlights the oversimplification of extrapolating from \ce{SO2} emissions to ERF using a simple linear relationship based on the global average, especially when emission locations shift aggressively throughout the industrial period and into the future. What is shown here is that the global and regional sensitivities of each process are highly heterogeneous, and a globally averaged sensitivity does not extrapolate across regions. 


It is essential to note some limitations and future improvements of this Chapter. Work in this Chapter has provided a quantitative framework for bounding this process, using a simplified framework to that in \citet{bellouinBoundingGlobalAerosol2020}.  Firstly, the rapid adjustments to aerosol cloud interaction were noted as the most difficult to bound on a global scale by \citet{bellouinBoundingGlobalAerosol2020}, but were omitted in this Chapter due to the time limit of this Thesis. In addition to aerosol-cloud-radiation process sensitivities, the dominant uncertainty in bounding aerosol radiative effect lies in the magnitude of industrial-era changes in AOD and CDNC \citep{bellouinBoundingGlobalAerosol2020}. 


Uncertainty in the estimate of radiative forcing from aerosol-cloud interactions arising from differences in parameter values across ESMs is an active area of investigation \citep[e.g.][]{ghanChallengesConstrainingAnthropogenic2016,bellouinBoundingGlobalAerosol2020}. The proposed framework for teasing out ERF uncertainty across models could be adopted to assist in model intercomparison. Especially for future aerosol emissions, in which emission locations are expected to shift away from East Asia and towards South Asia and Africa \citep{wilcoxRegionalAerosolModel2022}. This raises the question of ways to interpret and bound aerosol uncertainty in the era of CMIP7, which will feature more ESMs with prognostic sulfate aerosol formation and interactive chemistry \citep{dunneEvolvingCoupledModel2024,fiedlerAerChemMIP2NdashUnraveling2025}.
